Historical Precedents: Infrastructure Buildouts

"Those who cannot remember the past are condemned to repeat it." â€” George Santayana

History is littered with infrastructure buildout cycles that destroyed value:

  • 1990s Telecom (Fiber Optic): $500B invested, $2T+ in market cap destroyed. Infrastructure worked perfectly, but overcapacity led to bankruptcy wave.
  • 1970s Nuclear Power: Utility companies invested hundreds of billions expecting electricity demand growth. Demand flattened, leaving ratepayers and shareholders with stranded assets.
  • 2010s Oil & Gas (Shale): E&P companies spent $1.1T on shale infrastructure. Oversupply cratered prices, and most operators destroyed value despite production success.

The pattern: Revolutionary technology + valid demand thesis + competitive pressure to deploy capital + enthusiasm-driven overinvestment = destroyed value regardless of technology success. The technology works; the returns don't materialize because everyone builds simultaneously.

The Prisoner's Dilemma

No single company can stop spending without risking competitive disadvantage. Even if all players suspect collective overinvestment, each must continue or risk being left behind. This is classic game theory in actionâ€”and it rarely ends well.

Why this matters for investors: Even excellent execution at the company level may not translate to shareholder returns if industry-level dynamics are value-destructive. You can be right about the technology and wrong about the investment.

Europe is Leading, America is Following

The EU's Digital Markets Act (DMA) and Digital Services Act (DSA) represent the most aggressive tech regulation in history. Key provisions that went into effect in 2024-2025:

  • Interoperability requirements: Messaging platforms must allow cross-platform communication, weakening network effects
  • Data portability: Users can export data and take it to competitors more easily
  • Ban on self-preferencing: Platforms can't favor their own products in search/recommendations
  • Transparency in algorithms: Must disclose ranking criteria, reducing "secret sauce" advantages
  • Strict content moderation: Platforms liable for illegal content with 24-hour takedown requirements

These rules initially applied only to Europe (8% of global GDP), but American regulators are watching closely. If DMA proves effective, expect similar frameworks in the U.S. by 2027-2028.

AI Regulation: The Next Front

AI-specific regulation is coming faster than most expect. Key areas of concern:

Training Data Copyright

Lawsuits from publishers, artists, and musicians argue that training AI models on copyrighted content without permission/payment is infringement. If courts agree:

  • AI companies may owe billions in retroactive licensing fees
  • Future training costs could increase 10-50x
  • Open-source models may become legal liabilities
  • Incumbents with proprietary data (Microsoft, Google, Meta) would benefit; pure AI players would suffer

Liability for AI Outputs

Who's responsible when AI hallucinates false information that causes harm? When it generates copyrighted content? When it provides dangerous advice?

  • Section 230 protections (which shield platforms from user content liability) may not apply to AI-generated content
  • Strict liability regime would require expensive insurance and formal QA processes
  • Could make many consumer AI applications economically unviable

Energy and Environmental Restrictions

Training large AI models consumes gigawatts of power. As climate regulations tighten:

  • Carbon taxes could dramatically increase training costs
  • Data center build-outs may face permitting delays or denials
  • Water usage for cooling is already facing scrutiny in drought-prone regions
