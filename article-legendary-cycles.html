Act I

Crushing. Death. Chaos.

Markets work the same way ‚Äî except the physics are even more brutal. Because in markets, the exit doesn't just get crowded. It actually SHRINKS as more people try to use it.

The Corner's Doom Loop

Every corner fails for the same reasons. It's not bad luck. It's structural. The very act of cornering a market creates the conditions for your destruction:

1

Success Creates Visibility

Your corner starts working. Prices rise. But success creates headlines. Regulators notice. Exchanges notice. Enemies notice.

2

Size Becomes a Trap

Your position grows so large you CAN'T exit without crashing the market. You own 50% of supply ‚Äî who can you sell to?

3

Rules Get Changed

Exchanges raise margins. Position limits appear. "Liquidation only" trading starts. The game changes BECAUSE you were winning.

4

The Unwind Is Fatal

Forced to sell your massive position into a market you just cornered. Every sale crashes the price. Your exit is your funeral.

"A corner is easy to get into and impossible to get out of. It's like catching a tiger by the tail ‚Äî let go and it eats you."

üéØ The Setup

üíÄ The Aftermath

Why Bailouts Always Come After the Damage

Here's the cruelest truth about financial crises: help ALWAYS arrives. But it always arrives too late to save the victims.

The bailout comes. The system gets saved. The institutions survive. But the people? Their money is already gone. Their homes are already foreclosed. Their lives are already destroyed.

This isn't conspiracy. It's structural.

TOO LATE
Crisis Begins People Wiped Out Bailout Arrives

The 4 Reasons Bailouts Are Always Late

1

Recognition Lag

Authorities don't see the crisis until it's obvious. "Subprime is contained" ‚Äî Ben Bernanke, March 2007. It wasn't. By the time they admit it, damage is done.

2

Political Paralysis

Bailouts require votes. Votes require political will. Politicians don't want to "reward bad behavior" or "use taxpayer money." Debate takes months. Markets crash in hours.

3

Moral Hazard Fear

"If we bail them out, they'll do it again." This is TRUE. But while policymakers debate moral hazard, real people lose everything. Philosophy takes time. Margin calls don't wait.

4

The "Bodies Required" Threshold

The system only moves when systemic collapse is imminent. Not when 10,000 retail traders blow up. Not when small funds fail. Only when EVERYTHING is about to die.

The Bailout Timeline: Always the Same

STAGE 1: Denial
"The problem is contained"
Officials insist everything is fine. Markets continue crashing. Retail traders get margin called. Small institutions fail.
STAGE 2: Debate
"We need a measured response"
Meetings. Hearings. Op-eds. "We can't reward bad actors." Meanwhile, the fire spreads. Normal people lose jobs, homes, savings.
STAGE 3: Panic
"Oh god, it's all connected"
A major institution fails. Credit markets freeze. The domino effect begins. NOW they understand. But the damage is already catastrophic.
STAGE 4: Emergency Bailout
"Whatever it takes"
Trillions appear overnight. Banks get saved. Markets stabilize. The system survives. The institutions survive. The people? Already casualties.
STAGE 5: Aftermath
"Never again"
Investigations. Regulations. Solemn promises. Executives get bonuses. Taxpayers get the bill. Until the next time. There's always a next time.

"The Fed will always do the right thing ‚Äî after exhausting every other option, and only when it's too late for it to matter."

The Eternal Loop

So why does the cycle repeat? Why don't we learn? Why do the same patterns destroy wealth generation after generation?

Because human psychology never changes.

We change the technology. We change the regulations. We change the actors. But we can't change the wiring in our brains. The same fear and greed that drove tulip mania drives crypto pumps. The same hubris that sank the Hunt Brothers sank Bill Hwang.

Greed Overpowers Memory

10 years after a crash, survivors retire. New traders enter. They didn't FEEL the pain. Reading about 2008 isn't the same as living it. So they make the same bets.

FOMO Is Biological

Watching others get rich triggers primal envy. This isn't weakness ‚Äî it's evolution. Missing the mammoth hunt meant starvation. Missing the trade feels the same.

Leverage Is Intoxicating

1x gains are boring. 10x gains are addictive. Every generation rediscovers that leverage is magic on the way up ‚Äî and suicide on the way down.

"It's Different This Time"

The four most expensive words in finance. New technology makes the delusion MORE convincing. AI, crypto, internet ‚Äî each was "the exception." None were.
