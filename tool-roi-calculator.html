Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between simple ROI and annualized ROI?

Simple ROI = (Final Value - Initial Investment) / Initial Investment Ã— 100. It shows total return but ignores time. Annualized ROI adjusts returns to yearly basis for fair comparison across different time periods. Example: 50% return in 5 years = 8.4% annualized. Always use annualized ROI to compare investments with different holding periods. Simple ROI is misleading when comparing short vs long-term investments.

What is a good ROI percentage for investments?

Stock market average: 10-12% annually. Good ROI: 12-20%. Excellent ROI: 20%+. Below 7%: Consider safer alternatives like FDs. Context matters - real estate typically returns 8-12%, mutual funds 12-15%, stocks 15-20% (with volatility). Risk and ROI are correlated - higher ROI usually means higher risk. Don't chase unrealistic ROI promises (25%+ guaranteed) - they're likely scams. Compare your ROI against relevant benchmarks (Nifty for stocks, inflation for bonds).

Can ROI be negative?

Yes, negative ROI means you lost money. Example: Invested â‚¹1,00,000, now worth â‚¹80,000 = -20% ROI. Reasons for negative ROI: bad investment choice, poor timing (bought at peak), market crash, business failure, high fees eating returns. If ROI is negative for extended period (3+ years for stocks), consider: 1) Holding longer (if fundamentals are good), 2) Cutting losses and reallocating, 3) Averaging down (if you believe in recovery). Not all negative ROIs are permanent - markets are cyclical.

Should I include dividends when calculating ROI?

Yes! Total ROI = ((Final Value + Dividends Received - Initial Investment) / Initial Investment) Ã— 100. Ignoring dividends understates true returns. Example: Stock bought at â‚¹100, now â‚¹120, received â‚¹10 dividends = 30% return (not 20%). For mutual funds, choose growth option to auto-reinvest dividends. For stocks, manually add dividend income to final value. Dividends significantly boost long-term returns - 2-3% dividend yield compounds over decades.

What's the difference between ROI and CAGR?

ROI shows total return percentage. CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate) shows smoothed annual growth rate. CAGR accounts for compounding - more accurate for multi-year investments. Example: Investment grows from â‚¹1L to â‚¹2L in 5 years. ROI = 100%. CAGR = 14.87%. Use ROI for simple calculations. Use CAGR for: 1) Multi-year comparisons, 2) Mutual fund performance, 3) Business growth rates, 4) Any investment with compounding. CAGR is industry standard for reporting returns.

How do taxes and fees affect ROI?

Taxes and fees significantly reduce actual ROI. Calculate Post-Tax ROI = ((Final Value - Tax - Fees) - Initial Investment) / Initial Investment. Example: 15% pre-tax ROI becomes 11.25% post-tax after STCG/LTCG. Mutual fund expense ratio of 1-2% annually compounds into 15-25% lower returns over 20 years. Brokerage, STT, GST eat 0.5-1% of trading gains. Always compare investments on post-tax, post-fee basis. Tax-efficient investing (LTCG vs STCG, tax-loss harvesting) can boost net returns by 2-3% annually.
