Interpreting PCR:

  • PCR > 1.5: Excessive bearishness. Too many puts = contrarian bullish signal. Market likely to bounce.
  • PCR 1.2-1.5: Bearish sentiment. Moderate fear. Watch for support levels.
  • PCR 0.8-1.2: Neutral zone. No extreme positioning. Trade with technical analysis.
  • PCR 0.5-0.8: Bullish sentiment. Moderate greed. Watch for resistance levels.
  • PCR < 0.5: Excessive bullishness. Too many calls = contrarian bearish signal. Market likely to correct.

Understanding PCR:

Put-Call Ratio = Total Puts / Total Calls. PCR is a contrarian indicator. High PCR (>1.5) = too much fear = bullish signal (market tends to go up). Low PCR (<0.7) = too much greed = bearish signal (market tends to correct). OI-based PCR shows longer-term positioning. Volume-based PCR shows intraday sentiment. Professional traders use both. Nifty PCR typically ranges 0.8-1.3. Bank Nifty PCR is usually more volatile. Extreme readings (PCR > 2 or < 0.5) often mark market turning points. Use with price action - PCR confirms trend exhaustion.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Put-Call Ratio (PCR) and how do I use it?

PCR = Total Puts / Total Calls. It's a contrarian sentiment indicator. High PCR (>1.5) = market is too bearish = bullish signal (expect bounce). Low PCR (<0.7) = market is too bullish = bearish signal (expect correction). PCR works because crowds are usually wrong at extremes. When everyone buys puts (fear), market often goes up. When everyone buys calls (greed), market often drops. Use PCR to identify market extremes and potential turning points. Combine with price action - if Nifty is falling and PCR hits 1.8, expect reversal soon.

Should I use OI-based or Volume-based PCR?

Use both for different purposes. OI-based PCR: Shows accumulated positions over time. Better for swing trading and longer-term sentiment. More stable, less noisy. Volume-based PCR: Shows today's trading activity. Better for intraday trading. More volatile, captures immediate sentiment shifts. For Nifty/Bank Nifty expiry day trading, watch volume PCR. For weekly/monthly outlook, track OI PCR. Professional traders monitor both - if OI PCR is neutral (1.0) but volume PCR spikes to 1.5 intraday, it shows sudden fear that may reverse quickly. Divergences between the two signal sentiment changes.

Where do I find PCR data for Nifty and Bank Nifty?

Get PCR data from: 1) NSE website - download option chain CSV, sum put OI and call OI manually, 2) NSE pre-calculated PCR on their derivatives section (delayed), 3) Trading platforms like Sensibull, Opstra show live PCR, 4) Broker terminals (Zerodha, Upstox) have PCR indicators, 5) TradingView India VIX chart comments often discuss PCR levels. For real-time trading, use options analytics platforms. They calculate PCR automatically and show historical levels. Typical Nifty PCR range: 0.8-1.3. Bank Nifty PCR is more volatile: 0.7-1.5. Track daily to understand normal ranges before trading on extremes.

What PCR level signals a market reversal?

Reversal signals: PCR above 1.7-2.0 = extreme fear, market likely to bounce (buy signal). PCR below 0.6-0.5 = extreme greed, market likely to correct (sell signal). But context matters! During strong trends, PCR can stay extreme longer. In crashes, PCR can hit 2.5-3.0 before reversing. In euphoric rallies, PCR can drop to 0.4. Don't blindly counter-trade. Wait for price confirmation: PCR 1.8 + bullish candle + key support = strong buy. PCR 0.5 + bearish candle + resistance = strong sell. Best reversal trades: PCR extreme + opposite price action. Example: PCR 1.9 but Nifty makes higher high = peak fear exhaustion = buy.

Can I use PCR for intraday trading?

Yes! Volume-based PCR is excellent for intraday. Track PCR changes throughout the day: Morning high PCR (1.3) + market opens weak = short-covering rally likely. PCR drops from 1.1 to 0.7 by noon = shift to greed, expect afternoon correction. Sudden PCR spike (1.0 to 1.4 in 30 min) = panic buying of puts, fade the move. Expiry day: PCR extremes create violent reversals. PCR 1.8 at 1 PM on expiry = most puts will expire worthless, market likely pinned or up. For scalping: Wait for PCR to hit extremes (>1.5 or <0.6), then trade mean reversion. Works best in range-bound markets. In strong trends, PCR extremes can persist - use with caution.

How do I combine PCR with other indicators?

Powerful combinations: 1) PCR + India VIX: High PCR + high VIX = peak fear, strong reversal setup. Low PCR + low VIX = complacency before crash. 2) PCR + Max Pain: If PCR is 1.5 and max pain is 200 points higher, market likely to grind up. 3) PCR + Price action: PCR 1.8 + bullish engulfing at support = high probability long. 4) PCR + RSI: Oversold RSI + high PCR = double bottom setup. 5) PCR + Open Interest: Rising OI + high PCR = sustained fear, but if OI drops while PCR stays high = short covering imminent. Best strategy: PCR identifies sentiment extremes, technicals time the entry. Don't trade PCR alone - confirm with price.

What are common mistakes traders make with PCR?

Mistakes to avoid: 1) Counter-trading too early - PCR can stay extreme in strong trends. Wait for price confirmation. 2) Ignoring context - PCR 1.5 means different things in bull vs bear market. 3) Using only PCR - combine with technicals, don't trade blindly. 4) Not understanding normal ranges - PCR 1.1 is neutral for Nifty, not bearish. 5) Confusing OI and volume PCR - they measure different things. 6) Forgetting time decay - high PCR from put buying can reverse quickly as puts lose value. 7) Not adjusting for events - PCR naturally high before budget/elections (hedging), doesn't signal reversal. Learn normal PCR ranges for your instrument before trading extremes. Backtest your PCR levels before using real money.
