How Covered Calls Work

A covered call strategy involves owning shares and selling call options at a higher strike price. You earn premium income immediately. If stock stays below strike, you keep shares + premium. If stock rises above strike, shares get "called away" at strike price - you profit from premium + capital gain up to strike. Ideal for generating income from stocks you're willing to sell at the strike price.

Risks of Covered Calls

Capped Upside: Maximum profit limited to strike price + premium. Miss unlimited gains if stock surges. Still Exposed to Downside: Premium provides only small buffer. Stock can fall significantly. Assignment Risk: Forced to sell shares if called away, potentially during favorable long-term outlook. Opportunity Cost: Locked into position during option period. Best for stable/moderately bullish outlook, avoid during strong rallies.

A covered call is when you own 100+ shares of a stock and sell (write) call options against those shares. You receive premium upfront - this is immediate income. Which stocks are best for covered call strategy in India?

Best stocks for covered calls have high option liquidity and moderate volatility: Top picks: Reliance, TCS, Infosys, HDFC Bank, ICICI Bank, Bajaj Finance, Bharti Airtel, SBI, Nifty/Bank Nifty stocks. What strike price should I select for covered calls?

Strike selection balances income vs assignment risk: Conservative (OTM 5-10%): Strike 5-10% above current price. Lower premium (0.5-1.5% of stock price) but low assignment risk. Use when bullish, want to keep shares. Moderate (OTM 2-5%): Strike 2-5% above. Medium premium (1-2%). Balanced approach, most popular for consistent income. Aggressive (ATM/ITM): At or below current price. High premium (2-4%+) but high assignment probability. Use when ready to sell shares or very bearish. Example: Stock at â‚¹1000. Conservative: â‚¹1050-1100 strike, Moderate: â‚¹1020-1050 strike, Aggressive: â‚¹950-1000 strike. Monthly expiries typically offer best premium/time balance.

What happens when my covered call gets assigned?

Assignment means selling your shares at strike price: Process: On expiry, if stock > strike, your shares automatically sold at strike price. Buyer exercises their right to buy. Financial impact: Profit = (Strike - Original cost) + Premium received. You lose shares but realize capital gain + option income. Example: Bought 100 Infosys at â‚¹1400. Sold â‚¹1500 call for â‚¹30 premium. Stock goes to â‚¹1550. Assignment: Sell at â‚¹1500 (not â‚¹1550). Total profit: â‚¹100 gain + â‚¹30 premium = â‚¹13,000 on â‚¹1.4L investment = 9.3% in one month. What to do after: 1) Buy back shares if still bullish (at higher price), 2) Move to different stock, 3) Wait for pullback to re-enter. Assignment is not bad - you captured premium + targeted gain!

Should I roll my covered calls or let them expire?

Decision depends on stock outlook and option value: Let expire: Stock below strike: Option expires worthless, you keep premium + shares. Sell new calls next cycle for more income. Stock slightly above strike: Small profit, accept assignment or roll. Roll forward (buy back + sell further dated): Stock approaching strike, still bullish: Buy back current call (at loss), sell next month's call at higher strike. Extends duration, collects more premium. Stock surging past strike: Buy back call to avoid assignment, capture unlimited upside. Example: Sold â‚¹1500 call for â‚¹30, stock now â‚¹1520. Option worth â‚¹25. Roll: Buy back at â‚¹25 (â‚¹5 profit), sell next month â‚¹1550 call for â‚¹35 = collect another â‚¹10 premium. Use rolling when bullish, accept assignment when neutral/bearish.
